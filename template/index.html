Data Analysis


The Team will illustrate a rise in both population and household income of international minority groups, concentrated in specific U.S. markets, that we believe will increase the demand for U.S. based international K-12 schools.
International K-12 schools which opened several decades ago were established primarily to meet the needs of expatriate communities abroad. The expatriates were mostly employed at foreign embassies or multinational companies. Schools taught the national curricula of their countries of origin, usually British or American, to prepare students for a western education.
In developing countries, this education-career pathway has been followed by children of the elite for many years, but was unaffordable to most other families. However, rising prosperity in developing countries over the last several decades has changed this.
Many parents have had the means to give their children the chance to follow this path, and a significant number of students have attended leading U.S. universities and established career and families here.
However, the K12 performance of U.S. schools have declined over that same period where global rankings in Science, Math and Reading have fallen to 19th, 30th and 19th respectively. With support for charter schools declining, we expect the data to suggest a rise in U.S. International schools to fill this demand.
